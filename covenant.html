What it is

H3772 כָּרַת kârath, kaw-rath'; a primitive root; to cut (off, down or asunder); by implication, to destroy or consume; specifically, to covenant (i.e. make an alliance or bargain, originally by cutting flesh and passing between the pieces):—be chewed, be con-(feder-) ate, covenant, cut (down, off), destroy, fail, feller, be freed, hew (down), make a league (covenant), × lose, perish, × utterly, × want.

G1242 διαθήκη diathḗkē, dee-ath-ay'-kay; from G1303; properly, a disposition, i.e. (specially) a contract (especially a devisory will):—covenant, testament.

Think of a covenant as the making of a sacred agreement of which both party swears that they will not break.

There are the promises that each party commits in the agreement. In this covenant I swear that I will xxx. These promises can also called blessings.

Blessed be Jehovah who has given rest to His people Israel, according to all that He has spoken; not one word of all His good Word has fallen, which He spoke by the hand of His servant Moses. 1 Kings 8:56

There also are promises that each party makes that they will do if the other party breaks the covenant. If you break the covenant then I will hunt you down and kill you, and if I don’t kill you then my children will hunt down you and your children to kill you. These are also called curses.

When the covenant is made it takes place in what is called a ceremony. In the ceremony there is a “thing” that confirms that each party swears that they will not break the covenant. It can be giving the other party something that is owned by you that is treasured. This “Xxx” is most important to me so I am giving it to you so you know that I am serious about making this covenant. These are called tokens of the covenant.

What Paul said about it

But the promises were spoken to Abraham and to his Seed. It does not say, and to seeds [plural], as of many, but as of one [singular], “And to your Seed”, which is Christ.
And I say this, A covenant having been ratified before to Christ by God, the Law coming into being four hundred and thirty years after, does not annul the promise, so as to abolish it.
For if the inheritance is of Law, it is no more of promise; but God has given it to Abraham through promise.
Why the Law then? It was for the sake of transgressions, until the Seed should come, to whom it had been promised, being ordained through angels in a mediator's hand.
But the Mediator is not of one, but God is one.
Then is the Law against the promises of God? Let it not be! For if a law had been given which had been able to make alive, indeed righteousness would have been out of Law.

These promises led to several developments in Hebrew theology.

a) The City of God. David’s city, located on Mount Zion became the focus of soteriological and eschatological interest, rather than the whole of the Promised Land (Psalm 48:2; Psalm 133:3; Isaiah 2:2-4; Isaiah 51:3; Micah 4:1-4; cf. Hebrews 12:22; Hebrews 13:14; Revelation 21). It also led to the belief that Jerusalem would never fall (Isaiah 33:20-22; Jeremiah 26:1-19), a belief shattered by the conquest by Nebuchadnezzar of Babylon (Psalm 137:1-3; Micah 3:9-12).

b) The Messiah. The idea of an ideal Davidite is developed in the Psalms and the prophetic writings. This “anointed one” or “Messiah” would rule the nations from Zion and serve as a priest after the order of Melchizedek (Psalm 2; Psalm 110). Like his forefather he would be born in Bethlehem in Judah (Micah 5:2; cf. Matthew 2:1-6).

Christ redeemed us from the curse of the Law, having become a curse for us; for it has been written, “Cursed is everyone having been hung on a tree;” that the blessing of Abraham might be to the nations in Christ Jesus, that we might receive the promise of the Spirit through faith.
Brothers, I speak according to man, a covenant having been ratified, even among mankind, no one sets aside or adds to it.
But the promises were spoken to Abraham and to his Seed (it does not say, And to seeds, as of many, but as of one, “And to your Seed,” which is Christ).
And I say this, A covenant having been ratified before to Christ by God, the Law coming into being four hundred and thirty years after, does not annul the promise, so as to abolish it.
For if the inheritance is of Law, it is no more of promise; but God has given it to Abraham through promise. Ephesians 3:6-18
